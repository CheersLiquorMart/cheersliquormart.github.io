Food & Wine Pairing Guide

Master the basics of food pairing to help customers complete their meal plans.

Pairing Principles: The Basics

QUICK SELLING TIP

When a customer is purchasing wine for a dinner party, ask what they're serving. Offering a specific pairing recommendation demonstrates your expertise and can lead to higher-value purchases.

The Six Basic Tastes in Food

Understanding how these tastes interact with wine is fundamental to successful pairing:

  • Salt: Enhances wine body, reduces bitterness, and can make wines taste smoother.
  • Acid: High-acid foods need high-acid wines to match intensity.
  • Sweet: Wine should be as sweet or sweeter than the food, or it will taste flat.
  • Bitter: Can clash with high-tannin wines, creating excessive bitterness.
  • Fat: Rich, fatty foods pair well with tannic wines or high-acid wines to cut through the richness.
  • Umami: This savory taste can make wines taste flat or bitter; often needs wines with age or richness.

Three Approaches to Pairing

1. Congruent Pairing

Match similar flavors between the food and wine.

Example: Buttery lobster with a buttery Chardonnay

When to Use: When you want to amplify and enhance certain flavor characteristics

2. Complementary Pairing

Contrast flavors to create balance.

Example: Sweet dessert wine with salty blue cheese

When to Use: When you want to balance intense flavors or create complexity

3. Regional Pairing

"What grows together, goes together" - pair regional wines with cuisine from the same area.

Example: Italian Chianti with tomato-based pasta

When to Use: When suggesting authentic pairings that have stood the test of time

Perfect Meal Pairings

Suggest these complete pairings for common meal scenarios:

Steak Dinner

Main: Ribeye steak

Wine: Cabernet Sauvignon or Malbec

Why it works: The high protein and fat in steak pairs perfectly with the tannins in these bold reds, creating a smooth mouthfeel.

Upsell opportunity: Suggest a premium Napa Cabernet or Argentinian Malbec Reserva.

Seafood Feast

Main: Grilled salmon, shrimp, scallops

Wine: Pinot Noir (for salmon) or Albariño (for lighter seafood)

Why it works: Pinot's light body and acidity complement salmon's richness, while Albariño's crisp character enhances shellfish.

Upsell opportunity: Suggest a premium Oregon Pinot Noir or Spanish Albariño.

Italian Night

Main: Pasta with tomato sauce or pizza

Wine: Chianti Classico or Sangiovese

Why it works: The high acidity in these wines matches the tomato acidity, while their moderate tannins complement the richness.

Upsell opportunity: Suggest a Chianti Classico Riserva or Super Tuscan blend.

Spicy Asian Cuisine

Main: Thai curry, Indian dishes, Szechuan

Wine: Off-dry Riesling or Gewürztraminer

Why it works: The slight sweetness in these wines balances spicy heat, while their aromatic qualities complement complex spices.

Upsell opportunity: Suggest a premium German Spätlese Riesling.

Cheese Board

Main: Variety of cheeses

Wine: Versatile options like Champagne or medium-bodied reds

Why it works: Sparkling wines cleanse the palate between different cheeses; aromatic whites pair with soft cheeses; reds with firm cheeses.

Upsell opportunity: Suggest a true Champagne or aged Port for blue cheese.

BBQ & Grilling

Main: Ribs, pulled pork, grilled meats

Wine: Zinfandel, Syrah, or Malbec

Why it works: These fruit-forward, full-bodied wines stand up to strong BBQ flavors while complementing the smokiness.

Upsell opportunity: Suggest a premium old-vine Zinfandel or Barossa Shiraz.

Common Pairing Challenges & Solutions

Difficult-to-Pair Foods

  • Artichokes: Make wines taste sweeter; try Sauvignon Blanc or dry sparkling wine
  • Asparagus: Can make wine taste metallic; try grassy Sauvignon Blanc or unoaked Chardonnay
  • Eggs: Coat the palate and can make wines taste flat; try sparkling wine or crisp whites
  • Very Spicy Foods: Can overwhelm many wines; try off-dry Riesling or Gewürztraminer
  • Vinegar-Based Dressings: High acidity clashes with wine; try very high-acid wines like Sancerre

HANDLING MIXED PREFERENCES

When customers are serving multiple dishes or have guests with different preferences, suggest versatile wines that pair with a range of foods:

  • Sparkling wine: The acidity and bubbles make it food-friendly across many dishes
  • Pinot Noir: Light enough for fish, substantial enough for meat
  • Dry Rosé: Combines some of the characteristics of both red and white wines
  • Chenin Blanc: Ranges from dry to sweet, with good acidity

Pairing Conversation Guide

Use these questions to guide customers to the perfect pairing:

Questions to Ask Customers

  1. "What are you serving with this wine?" or "Is this for a special occasion?"
  2. "Do you prefer wines that contrast with your food or complement similar flavors?"
  3. "On a scale of 1-5, how adventurous are you with your wine choices?"
  4. "Are you looking for something that will please everyone, or are you open to something more unique?"
  5. "What's your price range for this bottle?" (Important to ask tactfully)

Suggested Responses for Common Scenarios

For a Special Occasion Dinner

Customer: "We're having friends over for a special dinner with beef tenderloin."

Your Response: "Beef tenderloin pairs beautifully with a medium to full-bodied red wine. A Cabernet Sauvignon would be classic, offering rich black fruit flavors and enough structure to complement the meat without overpowering its more delicate flavor compared to ribeye. Our [Premium Cabernet] at $45 would be perfect, but I can also suggest our [Mid-range option] at $28 if you prefer."

For a Casual Gathering

Customer: "We're having a casual pizza night with friends."

Your Response: "Pizza night calls for something fun and versatile. A medium-bodied red with good acidity works wonderfully with tomato sauce. I'd recommend an Italian Chianti or this California Zinfandel, which has bright fruit flavors and a touch of spice that will complement a variety of toppings. Both are crowd-pleasers that won't break the bank."

For a Customer Who Doesn't Know What They Want

Customer: "I'm not sure what wine to get. We're having salmon for dinner."

Your Response: "Salmon pairs wonderfully with several options depending on how it's prepared. A classic pairing would be Pinot Noir, which has enough body to stand up to the fish but won't overpower it. If it's served with a cream sauce, you might enjoy a lightly oaked Chardonnay. Or for something different, a dry rosé offers the best of both worlds. Would you like me to show you some options in these styles?"
