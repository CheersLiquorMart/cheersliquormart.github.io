Making Product Recommendations

Learn how to match customer preferences with the perfect product

One of the fun parts of working at Cheers Liquor Mart is helping customers find products they'll love. Whether it's picking a wine for dinner or a new beer to try, good recommendations can build customer trust and lead to repeat business. This guide will help you recommend products like a pro.

The Recommendation Process

  • Ask a Few Friendly Questions

    To give a good recommendation, quickly find out what the customer likes. Ask easy questions like:

    • "What kind of drinks do you usually enjoy?"
    • "Is this for a special occasion or just something for tonight?"
    • "Do you like sweeter or not sweet?"

    If they're buying a gift, you might ask, "Do you know if they prefer red wine or white wine?" These questions are short and help you figure out their taste preferences and purpose.

  • Listen and Use Their Clues

    Based on what they say, think of a product that fits. For example:

    • If a customer says, "I usually drink Coors Light," you know they like light, mild beer. You might recommend a similar light beer or a step up like a local golden ale (not something heavy like a stout!).
    • If they say, "I love Jack Daniels whiskey," you can suggest another Tennessee whiskey or a bourbon with a similar smooth, sweet profile, since Jack Daniels is a type of whiskey that's on the sweeter side due to charcoal filtering.
    • If they mention, "I prefer sweet wines," you'll steer towards Moscato, Port, or sweet Riesling, not a dry Chardonnay.

    Use what you see too: if they are browsing the craft beer fridge, they probably like craft beer. You can jump in with, "Looking for any style in particular? Our IPA section is here, and we have a new hazy IPA that's really juicy and popular," if they show interest in IPAs.

  • Recommend with Confidence and Clarity

    Once you have an idea, make your suggestion. Be clear and positive: "Based on what you've told me, I'd recommend this one – it's a medium red wine that's smooth, perfect if you normally like Merlot. I think you'll enjoy it."

    Give 1–2 reasons why you chose it (e.g. flavor, popularity, goes with their meal). Use simple terms like: fruity, dry, smooth, rich, light, crisp. Avoid over-technical language.

    The key is to match their preference: "You said you like sweet drinks – this cider has a nice apple sweetness you might love."

Best Practices by Category

Wines

If they don't know much about wine, start with basics. Ask red or white? Light or heavy?

  • For red wine: "A light red like Pinot Noir is easy to drink, a heavy red like Cabernet is more bold."
  • For white: "Chardonnay is smooth and not sweet (dry), while something like a Moscato is very sweet and fruity."

If it's for dinner pairing, use simple rules: red wine for red meat, white wine for fish or chicken (as a general tip).

Spirits

Find out if they sip it straight or mix it.

  • For sipping whiskey: you might suggest a nicer aged bourbon or single malt scotch if they want to try something special.
  • For mixing: a decent mid-range whiskey or vodka is fine. Example: "Making cocktails? Then you don't need the super expensive whiskey; this one will be great in a mixed drink and is affordable."

For tequila, you could mention silver vs. reposado differences simply (silver for mixing, reposado or añejo for sipping because it's smoother from aging).

Craft Beers

Ask if they prefer light, dark, or hoppy beers.

If they say "I don't know, what's an IPA?", explain: "IPA is a hoppier, more bitter beer with strong flavor. If you're new to craft beer, you might start with a lighter ale or a wheat beer which is smoother."

Recommend a popular local brew: "Our store's bestseller is this local pale ale – not too bitter, very refreshing."

Personal favorites help too, if you've tried them (it's okay to say, "This one's my favorite after work – give it a try!").

Key Phrases to Build Trust

"Our customers love..."

Example: "Our customers really love Brand Y Cabernet – it's one of our most popular reds. I think it'd be a safe bet."

People find comfort in knowing others liked it.

"One of my favorites is..."

If you have tried the product (and only if), you can say, "One of my favorites is this one, it has a nice vanilla note and super smooth finish."

Personal testimony can carry weight, but be honest – don't claim it if you haven't tried it.

"This one is a great value..."

Especially if price is a concern, you can point out, "This bottle gives you a lot of bang for your buck – great quality at a lower price."

Customers appreciate feeling like they found a good deal.

"Pairs well with..."

If they mentioned food, connect it: "This white wine pairs well with spicy food – it's crisp and will balance the heat."

Even if they didn't ask, a quick pairing tip makes you sound like an expert and the suggestion more appealing.

Always end a recommendation with a gentle prompt, like "How does that sound?" or "Do you want to give it a try?" This invites them to respond. They might say yes, or they might express a concern ("Not sure I want something sweet"). If they have a concern, you can adjust your recommendation accordingly.

Be Honest and Positive

When You Don't Know a Product Well

If you don't know a product well, focus on what you do know. For example, if a customer asks about a wine you haven't tried, you might say, "I haven't tried this one yet, but I know it's a dry red and it's from a very good winery. We've had a few customers say they enjoyed it."

Honesty builds trust. Never pretend to be an expert on something you're not – customers can tell. It's perfectly fine to use information from the bottle label or shelf tag ("It says here it has cherry and chocolate notes, which sounds delicious if that's the kind of flavor you enjoy").

Follow Up When Possible

This isn't always doable in a busy store, but if you happen to be around after the customer has decided, you can casually ask, "How did that bottle work out for you?" the next time you see them. If they loved it, great – you know you made a good recommendation (and they'll trust you even more). If not, you can say, "Sorry it wasn't to your taste. Let's try something different this time." This level of care turns one-time shoppers into regulars.

Remember that recommending products is like being a matchmaker between the customer and the huge variety of drinks in the store. You're helping them find "the one" they'll enjoy. Stay enthusiastic, keep learning about new products we get, and share that excitement with customers. When they walk out happy with a bottle you suggested, it's a rewarding feeling – for them and for you as a proud, knowledgeable employee.

Enjoy the process, and don't be afraid to keep improving your recommendations as you gain experience on the floor. Cheers to great service and happy customers!
