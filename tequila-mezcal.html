Tequila & Mezcal Education

Learn about these premium Mexican spirits to help customers discover authentic agave products.

What is Tequila?

Tequila is a Mexican spirit made from the blue agave plant, primarily produced in Jalisco and a few surrounding states. It has a rich cultural heritage and is regulated by strict production standards:

  • Must be made from at least 51% blue agave, though 100% agave tequila is superior.
  • Distilled in copper or stainless steel stills.
  • Aged in oak barrels or stainless steel, depending on the style.

QUICK SELLING TIP

When customers are looking at tequila, check the bottle for "100% Agave" labeling. If it doesn't say this, it's a mixto tequila. Gently educate customers on the difference and suggest 100% agave options for a better experience, especially if they complain about past bad experiences with tequila.

Types of Tequila

Blanco (Silver)
  • Unaged or aged less than 2 months in stainless steel or neutral oak.
  • Has the purest agave flavor – peppery, citrusy, and herbal.
  • Best for cocktails like margaritas.
Reposado (Rested)
  • Aged 2 months to 1 year in oak barrels.
  • Smoother than Blanco, with notes of vanilla, caramel, and light spice.
  • Great for sipping or premium cocktails.
Añejo (Aged)
  • Aged 1 to 3 years in oak barrels.
  • Develops richer flavors with oak, vanilla, dried fruit, and spice.
  • Best for sipping neat or on the rocks.
Extra Añejo (Ultra-Aged)
  • Aged over 3 years in oak barrels.
  • Deep, complex flavors with chocolate, tobacco, and dried fruit notes.
  • Premium sipping tequila, similar to aged whiskey.
Joven (Gold)
  • A mix of Blanco and aged tequilas, or sometimes Blanco with added caramel color.
  • Typically lower quality, often used for budget cocktails.

100% Agave vs. Mixto

100% Agave Tequila

Made only from blue agave, offering better taste and quality.

  • Cleaner flavor profile
  • Less likely to cause hangovers
  • Must be bottled in Mexico
  • Will always state "100% Agave" on the label
Mixto Tequila

Contains at least 51% agave, with added sugars and coloring. Usually cheaper and lower quality.

  • Can contain artificial flavors and coloring
  • Often associated with harsh taste and strong hangovers
  • Does not state "100% Agave" on the label
  • More commonly used in budget bars

The Tequila-Making Process

Understanding how tequila is made helps explain the flavor differences between brands and styles.

1

Harvesting (Jimado)

Agave plants take 6-8 years to mature. The piña (heart of the plant) is harvested by skilled workers called jimadores, who remove the spiny leaves with a special tool called a coa.

2

Cooking

The piñas are slowly roasted in brick ovens or autoclaves (pressure cookers) to convert starches into fermentable sugars. This process can take 1-3 days in traditional ovens or just 8-12 hours in modern autoclaves.

3

Extraction

The cooked agave is crushed to release its sweet juice, known as "mosto". Traditional methods use a large stone wheel called a tahona, while modern distilleries use mechanical shredders and roller mills.

4

Fermentation

Yeast is added to convert sugars into alcohol over 2-5 days. This produces a low-alcohol liquid called "tepache" (7-10% ABV). The type of yeast and fermentation conditions greatly affect flavor.

5

Distillation

Tepache is distilled twice in copper or stainless-steel stills. The first distillation produces a low-proof spirit called "ordinario," and the second results in clear, high-proof Blanco tequila (55-60% ABV, usually diluted to 40%).

6

Aging

If aging is required (for Reposado, Añejo, or Extra Añejo), tequila is stored in oak barrels, developing deeper flavors. Most producers use ex-bourbon barrels, though some use wine or cognac barrels for unique flavor profiles.

UPSELLING OPPORTUNITY

When recommending premium tequilas, mention production details: "This tequila is made using traditional brick ovens and a tahona stone to crush the agave, resulting in a richer, more complex flavor than mass-produced brands." This knowledge helps justify the higher price point.

Mezcal vs. Tequila

While all tequilas are technically mezcals, not all mezcals are tequilas. Here are the key differences between these related but distinct spirits:

Feature Tequila Mezcal
Agave Type Only Blue Agave (Agave tequilana Weber) Up to 30+ agave varieties (Espadin is most common)
Production Often industrialized in large facilities More artisanal, small-batch methods
Cooking Steam-cooked in ovens/autoclaves Roasted in underground pits with wood and stones
Flavor Clean, smooth, agave-forward Smoky, earthy, complex, often more rustic
Regions Jalisco + a few states (limited by law) Oaxaca + 8 other states

What is Mezcal?

Mezcal is an umbrella term for all agave spirits, with tequila as a subcategory. It's production is characterized by:

  • It is mainly produced in Oaxaca, but also in Durango, Guerrero, and six other states.
  • The traditional production method involves roasting agave piñas in underground pits, giving mezcal its signature smoky flavor.
  • Mezcal is often small-batch and artisanal, with each batch reflecting the unique agave variety, climate, and production method.
  • Many producers still use traditional clay pot stills rather than copper or stainless steel.

Types of Mezcal

Like tequila, mezcal is categorized by aging:

Joven (Young)

Unaged, capturing pure agave flavors with the strongest smoke character. Most traditional and representative of mezcal's distinctive style.

Reposado

Aged 2-12 months, offering a smoother experience with light oak influence while retaining the mezcal's smoky character.

Añejo

Aged over 1 year, developing rich and complex flavors with more pronounced wood notes complementing the smoke.

SELLING TIP

For customers interested in trying mezcal for the first time, describe it as "tequila's smokier cousin." Suggest they start with a cocktail like a Mezcal Margarita or a Mezcal Negroni before trying it neat, as the smoke can be intense for beginners.

Serving & Drinking Tequila & Mezcal

Advising customers on proper serving methods enhances their experience and appreciation of these spirits:

Tequila Serving Guide

Sipping Tequilas:

Añejo, Extra Añejo, and high-end Reposado expressions are best enjoyed neat or with minimal dilution.

Cocktail Tequilas:

Blanco and mid-tier Reposado work best in mixed drinks, with enough character to stand up to other ingredients.

Neat or On the Rocks:

Serve in a lowball glass at room temperature or with a large ice cube for slight cooling without excessive dilution.

Traditional Mexican Style:

Sipped alongside sangrita (a spiced citrus chaser) or with lime and salt (though quality tequilas don't need these additions).

Glassware Recommendation:

Stemmed snifters concentrate aromas, while small tumblers or specialized tequila flutes also work well for sipping.

Mezcal Serving Guide

Traditional Service:

Best enjoyed neat in small clay copitas or shallow jicaras to appreciate its complexity and rustic character.

Accompaniments:

Often served with orange slices and sal de gusano (worm salt made with ground larvae, chilies, and salt) for an authentic experience.

Cocktail Usage:

Works in cocktails, but the smoky flavor dominates - use sparingly as an accent or in smoke-forward cocktails like mezcal old fashioneds.

Temperature:

Always serve at room temperature - chilling mutes the complex flavors that make mezcal special.

Ritual Aspect:

Traditionally shared in a communal setting - emphasize the cultural significance and craftsmanship behind each bottle.

UPSELLING OPPORTUNITY

When customers purchase premium tequila or mezcal, suggest appropriate glassware and accompaniments: "To fully enjoy this special bottle at home, you might want to consider proper glassware - we have these beautiful handcrafted copitas that really enhance the aromas."

Popular Tequila & Mezcal Cocktails

  • Margarita: The classic tequila cocktail - tequila, orange liqueur, and lime juice.
  • Paloma: Tequila and grapefruit soda - more popular in Mexico than margaritas.
  • Mezcal Negroni: Substituting mezcal for gin creates a smoky twist on the classic.
  • Oaxaca Old Fashioned: Combines tequila, mezcal, agave nectar, and bitters for a complex sipper.
  • Ranch Water: Simple highball with tequila, lime juice, and Topo Chico mineral water.
  • Mexican Mule: Tequila version of a Moscow Mule with ginger beer and lime.
