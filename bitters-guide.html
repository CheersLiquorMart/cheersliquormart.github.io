Understanding Bitters

The secret ingredients that elevate cocktails from good to exceptional

RETAIL SELLING TIP

When a customer purchases a premium spirit, suggest a complementary bitter as an affordable way to create multiple cocktail variations at home. For example, "This bourbon would make an excellent Old Fashioned with our Angostura bitters—one bottle lasts for months and gives you dozens of cocktails."

What Are Bitters?

Definition: Bitters are highly concentrated flavoring agents made by infusing a high-proof spirit with a blend of botanicals—herbs, spices, roots, barks, and fruits.

Selling Points

  • Not a Standalone Drink: They're designed to be added by the dash, not sipped on their own.
  • Historical Heritage: Originally developed in the 19th century (think Angostura from 1824) as medicinal tonics, bitters evolved into the secret ingredient that transforms raw spirits into perfectly balanced cocktails.
  • Flavor Enhancer: Just as salt elevates food, a few drops of bitters bring harmony by balancing sweetness and acidity, deepening a cocktail's overall profile.

Types of Bitters

Aromatic Bitters

Examples: Angostura, Peychaud's

Sales Angle: These are the workhorses of cocktail bars—customers can't go wrong with a classic for Old Fashioneds or Manhattans.

Citrus Bitters

Examples: Orange bitters, lemon bitters

Sales Angle: Perfect for brightening up gin and vodka cocktails without overpowering them, offering a fresh burst of citrus flavor.

Herbal Bitters

Examples: Bitters infused with mint, thyme, or rosemary

Sales Angle: Ideal for modern, botanical-forward drinks. They add a garden-fresh note that appeals to craft cocktail enthusiasts.

Spiced & Nut Bitters

Examples: Chocolate bitters, walnut bitters, celery bitters

Sales Angle: Offer a bold twist that reinvents classic recipes. They're great for customers looking to experiment and add a unique flair to their cocktails.

How to Use Bitters

  • Start Small: Recommend starting with 1–2 dashes and adjusting to taste—this teaches customers the proper technique without overwhelming the drink.
  • Integration Tips: Explain that bitters should be mixed into the cocktail's base ingredients or used as a garnish to tie together sweet, sour, and salty elements. For instance, a dash of orange bitters in a gin and tonic enhances the fresh lime notes.
  • Enhance, Don't Overpower: Emphasize that the goal is to complement the cocktail, not mask its components.

Classic Cocktails Using Bitters

  • Old Fashioned: Whiskey, sugar, Angostura bitters
  • Manhattan: Rye whiskey, sweet vermouth, Angostura bitters
  • Sazerac: Rye whiskey, sugar, Peychaud's bitters, absinthe rinse
  • Champagne Cocktail: Champagne, sugar cube soaked in Angostura bitters
  • Martini: Gin, dry vermouth, often with a dash of orange bitters

Best Practices and Tips for Bitters

  • Quality Matters: Advise customers to choose reputable brands like Angostura or Peychaud's. Though they may cost slightly more, the depth and consistency of flavor are worth the investment.
  • Experiment Cautiously: Encourage trial by swapping a type of bitters (e.g., celery or chocolate bitters in an Old Fashioned) to create a signature twist.
  • Proper Storage: Suggest storing bitters in a cool, dark place to maintain their flavor integrity over time.
  • Cross-Selling Opportunities: When a customer is buying whiskey, always suggest Angostura bitters for Old Fashioneds. For gin purchases, suggest orange bitters to enhance martinis.
  • Highlight Value: Despite their higher price points, remind customers that bitters are used by the dash—one bottle can last for months or even years of regular cocktail making, making them an extremely cost-effective purchase.
