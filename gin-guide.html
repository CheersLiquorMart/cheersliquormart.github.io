Gin Guide

Master the essentials of gin to provide expert recommendations to your customers

What is Gin?

Gin is a distilled spirit where juniper is non-negotiable. It starts with a neutral spirit (usually from grains) that's transformed by a mix of botanicals. The taste profile is built around juniper, with support from citrus, coriander, and herbs.

Key Features of Gin

  • Juniper Dominance: It must have juniper as the primary flavor.
  • Botanical Blend: Additional ingredients like citrus peel, angelica root, and cardamom define its character.
  • Versatility: Ranges from dry and piney to sweet and floral.

Types of Gin & Their Best Uses

London Dry Gin

  • No added sugar
  • Pure, juniper-forward taste with citrus and herbal notes
  • Classic examples: Tanqueray, Beefeater, Bombay Sapphire

Best for: Classic cocktails like a crisp Gin & Tonic or Martini. Its strong juniper bite stands up well to mixers.

Plymouth Gin

  • Made exclusively in Plymouth, England
  • Smoother and earthier
  • Works perfectly in a martini or gin & tonic

Best for: Cocktails that benefit from a smoother, slightly sweeter profile—ideal for Negronis and Gimlets.

Old Tom Gin

  • A touch sweeter due to a slight sugar addition
  • Popular in 18th- and 19th-century recipes
  • Examples: Hayman's Old Tom

Best for: Vintage cocktails (think Tom Collins or Martinez) that need a touch of sweetness to balance the bitter or tart ingredients.

New Western Dry Gin

  • Shifts the focus away from heavy juniper
  • Emphasizes unique botanicals—floral, citrus, herbal, or even spicy
  • Examples: Hendrick's (with cucumber and rose), Aviation

Best for: Modern, creative cocktails that highlight unique botanicals. They're less juniper-heavy, offering versatility.

Genever

  • Gin's old-school ancestor
  • Examples: Bols Genever

Best for: Sipping neat or mixing in cocktails where a maltier, fuller flavor is welcome.

Navy Strength Gin

  • Higher alcohol content

Best for: Cocktails that need a punch. Works well when you want the gin's character to shine through without dilution.

Barrel-Aged Gin

  • Aged in wooden barrels, adding complexity

Best for: Sipping neat or using in cocktails that call for extra complexity. The wood notes add a rich depth similar to some whiskies.

The Gin-Making Process

  1. Base Spirit Production: Distill a neutral grain spirit (wheat, barley, or corn).
  2. Botanical Infusion: Either steep botanicals in the spirit or pass vapors through them for flavor extraction.
  3. Redistillation: The infused spirit is redistilled to blend and refine the flavors.
  4. Dilution & Bottling: Dilute the spirit to the desired strength (typically 40–50% ABV) and bottle.

How to Serve Gin

  • Classic Cocktails: Gin & Tonic, Martini, Negroni, and Tom Collins prove its versatility.
  • Neat or On the Rocks: Premium gins can be sipped like a fine whiskey.
  • Garnishes: Lemon, lime, cucumber, or even extra juniper can heighten the flavor profile.

QUICK SELLING TIP

When suggesting gin cocktails, ask customers if they prefer traditional juniper-forward gins or something with unique botanicals. This helps tailor recommendations to their taste preferences and demonstrates your expertise.
