Wine Terminology Guide

Understand common wine terms that will help you sound knowledgeable with customers.

Why Terminology Matters

Using the right wine terms builds credibility with customers and helps guide them to wines they'll enjoy. This guide will help you speak confidently about wine, understand what customers are asking for, and make precise recommendations.

COMMUNICATION TIP

When customers use wine terms incorrectly, avoid correcting them directly. Instead, incorporate the correct term naturally in your response: "Yes, that Cabernet does have nice tannins that give it structure. Let me show you some similar full-bodied reds you might enjoy."

Wine Tasting Vocabulary

These are the essential terms used to describe how a wine looks, smells, and tastes:

Appearance Terms

  • Body
    The perceived weight and texture of wine in your mouth. Described as light, medium, or full.
    Usage: "This Chardonnay has a full body with a creamy texture."
  • Clarity
    How clear or transparent a wine appears: brilliant, clear, hazy, or cloudy.
    Usage: "Notice the brilliant clarity of this Riesling when held against the light."
  • Legs/Tears
    The streaks that form on the glass after swirling the wine, indicating alcohol content and/or sweetness.
    Usage: "The slow, thick legs on this Cabernet suggest a higher alcohol content."

Aroma & Bouquet Terms

  • Aroma
    The smell derived from the grape variety itself.
    Usage: "This Sauvignon Blanc has bright citrus and grassy aromas."
  • Bouquet
    The smell that develops after fermentation and aging.
    Usage: "This aged Bordeaux has developed a complex bouquet with leather and tobacco notes."
  • Nose
    The combined smell of the wine, encompassing both aroma and bouquet.
    Usage: "This wine has a powerful nose with dark fruit and spice."

Taste & Mouthfeel Terms

  • Acidity
    The tartness or crispness of a wine that makes it refreshing and food-friendly.
    Usage: "This Pinot Grigio has bright acidity that makes it great with seafood."
  • Tannins
    Compounds that create a drying, astringent sensation, primarily in red wines.
    Usage: "This young Cabernet has firm tannins that will soften with age."
  • Finish
    The taste and sensation that lingers after swallowing the wine.
    Usage: "This Syrah has a long, peppery finish that continues well after you swallowed."
  • Balance
    When all components (fruit, acidity, tannin, alcohol) are in harmony.
    Usage: "What I love about this Pinot Noir is its perfect balance between fruit and acidity."
  • Structure
    The framework of a wine created by acidity, tannin, alcohol, and sugar.
    Usage: "This Barolo has the structure to age beautifully for decades."
  • Complexity
    The range and depth of flavors and aromas in a wine.
    Usage: "This aged Burgundy shows remarkable complexity with layers of fruit, earth, and spice."

Wine Production Terms

Understanding these terms will help you explain how wines are made:

Vineyard & Growing
  • Terroir
    (tehr-WAHR)
    The complete natural environment where a wine is produced, including soil, climate, and topography.
  • Old Vine
    Wines made from older grape vines (typically 30+ years old), often with more concentrated flavors.
  • Estate Bottled
    Wine made entirely from grapes grown on land owned or controlled by the winery.
Winemaking
  • Fermentation
    The process where yeast converts grape sugar into alcohol and CO2.
  • Maceration
    Soaking grape skins with the juice to extract color, tannins, and aromas.
  • Malolactic Fermentation
    A secondary fermentation that converts sharp malic acid to softer lactic acid, giving wines (especially Chardonnay) a buttery character.
Aging
  • Barrel-Aged
    Wine matured in oak barrels, which imparts flavors and allows minimal oxygen exposure.
  • New Oak vs. Neutral Oak
    New oak barrels impart strong flavors; neutral (previously used) barrels have more subtle effects.
  • Sur Lie
    (sir LEE)
    Aging wine on the dead yeast cells ("lees"), adding complexity and a creamy texture.

New World Wine Terms

  • AVA (American Viticultural Area)
    Officially designated wine grape-growing regions in the United States.
  • Single Vineyard
    Wine made exclusively from grapes grown in one specific vineyard, often indicating higher quality.
  • Reserve
    In the U.S., an unregulated term generally implying higher quality, but with no legal requirements.
  • Meritage
    (MEHR-ih-tij)
    A proprietary term for American Bordeaux-style blends.

CUSTOMER TIP

When customers find European wine labels confusing, explain that they typically emphasize place over grape variety. For example: "This wine is labeled 'Chablis' which means it's a Chardonnay from the Chablis region of France."

Decanting & Aeration
  • Decanting
    Pouring wine from the bottle into another container to separate it from sediment and/or expose it to oxygen.
  • Breathing
    Allowing wine to be exposed to air before serving to develop aromas and flavors.
  • Aerator
    A device that introduces air into wine as it's poured, speeding up the breathing process.
Glassware
  • Bordeaux Glass
    Tall with a broad bowl, designed for full-bodied reds like Cabernet Sauvignon.
  • Burgundy Glass
    Broader bowl to capture the aromas of delicate reds like Pinot Noir.
  • Flute
    Tall, narrow glass that preserves bubbles in sparkling wine.

Common Wine Flaws

Know these terms to identify and explain when a wine isn't showing its best:

  • Corked
    Wine contaminated by a chemical called TCA from the cork, giving a musty, wet cardboard smell. Not related to cork fragments in the wine.
  • Oxidized
    Wine that's been exposed to too much oxygen, appearing brownish with stale, nutty flavors similar to sherry.
  • Brett
    Short for Brettanomyces, a yeast that can produce flavors from spicy and leathery (sometimes desirable) to barnyard and band-aid (undesirable).
  • Volatile Acidity (VA)
    Excessive acetic acid giving a vinegar-like smell or taste.
  • Cooked
    Wine damaged by heat exposure, showing stewed fruit flavors and a flattened profile.

HANDLING CUSTOMER RETURNS

If a customer returns a wine believing it's flawed, avoid questioning their judgment. Instead say: "I'm sorry you didn't enjoy that bottle. Let's find something you'll like better." Then discreetly check the wine for flaws after they leave.
