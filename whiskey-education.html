Whisk(e)y Education Sheet

What is Whisk(e)y?

Whisky vs. Whiskey

  • "Whisky" (no 'e') – Used in Scotland, Canada, and Japan.
  • "Whiskey" (with 'e') – Used in Ireland and the United States.
  • General rule: If the country has an 'e' in its name (Ireland, United States), they spell it 'whiskey'. If not (Scotland, Canada, Japan), they spell it 'whisky'.

Whiskey is made from grains and oak.

  • Common grains: barley, corn, wheat, and rye (rarely, rice).
  • The type of grain, distillation process, barrel type, and aging time determine the whiskey's flavor and style.

Scotch Whisky

  • Must be distilled, matured, and bottled in Scotland.
  • Must be aged in oak barrels for at least three years.
  • No sugar or artificial coloring allowed.

Single Malt Scotch

  • Made only from malted barley.
  • Must be distilled in pot stills at a single distillery.
  • Typically a blend of different ages to balance flavors.
  • Peat: A plant-based fuel used to dry barley, giving some Scotch a smoky flavor.
  • Aged mostly in used American whiskey barrels rather than new oak.

American Whiskeys

Bourbon

  • Can be made anywhere in the U.S., though Kentucky produces most of it.
  • Must be at least 51% corn.
  • Aged in new charred oak barrels.
  • No sugar or artificial coloring allowed.

Tennessee Whiskey

  • Must be made in Tennessee.
  • Must be at least 51% corn.
  • Must go through the Lincoln County Process (filtered through maple charcoal before aging) for smoothness.

Rye Whiskey

  • Must be at least 51% rye.
  • Has a spicy, bold flavor.

Bulleit Bourbon (Example)

  • Higher rye content than most bourbons (68% corn, 28% rye, 4% malted barley).
  • Bold, spicy character with nutmeg, maple, oak, and toffee notes.

Irish Whiskey

  • Must be distilled and aged in Ireland for at least three years.
  • Typically made with unmalted barley and triple distilled, creating a smoother whiskey.

Types of Irish Whiskey

  • Pot Still Irish Whiskey: Mix of malted and unmalted barley, distilled two or three times in pot stills.
  • Malt Irish Whiskey: 100% malted barley, distilled in pot stills twice or three times.
  • Grain Irish Whiskey: Made from malted barley and other grains (e.g., corn, wheat), distilled in column stills.
  • Blended Irish Whiskey: A mix of two or more of the above.

Japanese Whisky

  • Inspired by Scottish production methods.
  • Uses column stills for grain whiskey and pot stills for malt whiskey.
  • Known for a clean, refined flavor with less cereal grain intensity than Scotch.
  • Must be fermented, distilled, and bottled in Japan.
  • Aging must occur in Japan.
  • Some distilleries experiment with non-traditional grains like rice (e.g., Kikori).

Canadian Whisky

  • Must be at least 40% ABV, made from cereal grains, and aged in wooden barrels for at least three years.
  • Has fewer restrictions than U.S. whiskey.
  • Grains are typically distilled separately and blended after aging.
  • Uses a high-strength "base whisky" (often corn-based) blended with "flavoring whisky" (often rye-based).

Crown Royal Deluxe (Example)

  • Created in 1939 for King George VI's visit to Canada.
  • Made with five different mash bills and 12 distillation blends.
  • Smooth with hints of vanilla, oak, butterscotch, and clove.
  • Ideal for mixing.

The Basic Process of Whiskey Production

1. Preparation

  • Grains are cleaned, milled, and cooked to make their starches accessible.

2. Mashing

  • Cooked grains are mixed with hot water, converting starches into fermentable sugars, creating a liquid called "mash".

3. Fermentation

  • Yeast is added to the mash in fermentation tanks, converting sugars into alcohol over 3-4 days.
  • The resulting liquid, known as "distiller's beer" or "wash", is about 10% alcohol.

4. Distillation

  • The wash is heated in pot or column stills, separating alcohol from water and impurities.
  • The result is a clear, high-proof spirit.

5. Aging

  • The spirit is aged in wooden barrels, where it absorbs flavors and color over time.
  • The type of barrel and length of aging greatly influence the final flavor.
