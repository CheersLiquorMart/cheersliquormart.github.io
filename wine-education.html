Wine Types & Regions

Learn the major wine varieties, regions, and what makes them unique.

Wine Basics: Understanding the Fundamentals

QUICK SELLING TIP

When recommending wine to customers, ask about their preferences for sweetness, body, and food pairings. This shows expertise and helps guide them to wines they'll enjoy at higher price points.

Wine Characteristics

Understanding these key elements will help you describe and sell wines more effectively:

Characteristic Description Spectrum
Body How the wine feels in your mouth; its weight and fullness Light → Medium → Full
Sweetness The amount of residual sugar left after fermentation Dry → Off-dry → Sweet
Acidity The tartness or crispness of the wine Low → Medium → High
Tannins Compounds that create dryness and astringency (primarily in red wines) Soft → Medium → Firm
Alcohol Measured as % ABV (alcohol by volume) Low (under 10%) → Medium (10-13.5%) → High (above 13.5%)

Red Wine Varieties

Red wines get their color from fermenting with grape skins. They generally have more tannins and often pair well with red meats and rich dishes.

Cabernet Sauvignon

Profile: Full-bodied with firm tannins and notes of black currant, blackberry, cedar, and sometimes green pepper.

Key Regions: Bordeaux (France), Napa Valley (California), Coonawarra (Australia)

Food Pairings: Steak, lamb, aged cheeses

Selling Point: "The king of red wines" - recognized globally as a premium variety

Merlot

Profile: Medium to full-bodied with soft tannins and flavors of plum, black cherry, and chocolate.

Key Regions: Bordeaux (France), Washington State, Tuscany (Italy)

Food Pairings: Roasted chicken, pork, tomato-based pasta dishes

Selling Point: Approachable and smooth - perfect for customers new to red wine

Pinot Noir

Profile: Light to medium-bodied with delicate tannins and notes of red berries, mushroom, and earth.

Key Regions: Burgundy (France), Willamette Valley (Oregon), Central Otago (New Zealand)

Food Pairings: Salmon, duck, mushroom dishes

Selling Point: "The heartbreak grape" - difficult to grow but produces some of the world's most sought-after wines

Syrah/Shiraz

Profile: Full-bodied with medium to high tannins and flavors of blackberry, pepper, and smoke.

Key Regions: Rhône Valley (France), Barossa Valley (Australia), Washington State

Food Pairings: Grilled meats, game, barbecue

Selling Point: Same grape, different names - Syrah (French style) tends to be more restrained, while Shiraz (Australian style) is often bolder and fruitier

Zinfandel

Profile: Medium to full-bodied with moderate tannins and jammy flavors of blackberry, raspberry, and spice.

Key Regions: California (especially Sonoma and Lodi)

Food Pairings: Pizza, burgers, spicy foods

Selling Point: "America's heritage grape" - with a rich history in California wine production

White Wine Varieties

White wines are typically fermented without grape skins, resulting in lighter colors and generally less tannins than reds.

Chardonnay

Profile: Medium to full-bodied with flavors ranging from apple and citrus to tropical fruit.

Style Variation: Unoaked (crisp, mineral) vs. Oaked (buttery, vanilla notes)

Key Regions: Burgundy (France), California, Australia

Food Pairings: Seafood, poultry, creamy sauces

Selling Point: Extremely versatile grape that can produce vastly different styles depending on winemaking choices

Sauvignon Blanc

Profile: Light to medium-bodied, high acidity, with flavors of grapefruit, green apple, and grass/herbs.

Key Regions: Loire Valley (France), Marlborough (New Zealand), California

Food Pairings: Goat cheese, salads, white fish

Selling Point: Crisp and refreshing - perfect for warm weather and light meals

Riesling

Profile: Light-bodied with high acidity and flavors of green apple, peach, and sometimes petrol notes.

Style Variation: Ranges from bone dry to very sweet

Key Regions: Germany, Alsace (France), Finger Lakes (New York)

Food Pairings: Spicy Asian cuisine, pork, seafood

Selling Point: Incredible versatility and aging potential - can develop complex flavors over decades

Pinot Grigio/Pinot Gris

Profile: Light-bodied with crisp acidity and delicate flavors of pear, apple, and mineral notes.

Style Variation: Italian Pinot Grigio (lighter, crisper) vs. Alsatian Pinot Gris (richer, fuller)

Key Regions: Northern Italy, Alsace (France), Oregon

Food Pairings: Light seafood, salads, appetizers

Selling Point: Approachable and easy-drinking - a crowd-pleasing option for diverse gatherings

Major Wine Regions of the World

France

The traditional benchmark for fine wine production with a focus on terroir (sense of place).

  • Bordeaux: Famous for red blends based on Cabernet Sauvignon and Merlot
  • Burgundy: The pinnacle of Pinot Noir and Chardonnay production
  • Champagne: The original and most prestigious sparkling wine region
  • Rhône Valley: Known for Syrah-based reds in the north and blends in the south
  • Loire Valley: Diverse region producing excellent Sauvignon Blanc, Chenin Blanc, and more

What Makes It Unique: Strict appellation system, centuries of winemaking tradition, and an emphasis on expressing the unique qualities of each specific vineyard site.

Italy

Incredible diversity with more native grape varieties than any other country.

  • Tuscany: Home to Chianti, Brunello di Montalcino, and Super Tuscans
  • Piedmont: Producer of age-worthy Barolo and Barbaresco from Nebbiolo grapes
  • Veneto: Known for Prosecco, Soave, and rich Amarone della Valpolicella

What Makes It Unique: Food-friendly wines designed to complement the local cuisine, with incredible regional diversity from north to south.

United States

New World innovation combined with quality-focused production.

  • Napa Valley: World-class Cabernet Sauvignon and Chardonnay
  • Sonoma: Diverse region excelling in Pinot Noir, Zinfandel, and Chardonnay
  • Willamette Valley: Oregon's premier Pinot Noir region
  • Washington State: Emerging region for Cabernet Sauvignon, Merlot, and Syrah

What Makes It Unique: Innovative techniques, lack of rigid traditions allowing for experimentation, and a focus on varietal expression.

Spain

Outstanding value with a mix of traditional and modern approaches.

  • Rioja: Known for age-worthy Tempranillo-based reds with American oak influence
  • Ribera del Duero: Powerful Tempranillo wines from a high-altitude region
  • Priorat: Intense, mineral-driven reds from difficult mountainous terrain

What Makes It Unique: Some of the longest aging requirements for their classification system, extensive use of oak aging, and exceptional value compared to other European regions.

Australia

Bold, fruit-forward wines with distinctive regional character.

  • Barossa Valley: Rich, powerful Shiraz
  • Margaret River: Premium Cabernet Sauvignon and Chardonnay
  • Hunter Valley: Distinctive aged Semillon wines

What Makes It Unique: Diverse climate zones allowing for a wide range of grape varieties, innovative winemaking, and a focus on regional distinctiveness.

Wine Label Reading Guide

Understanding wine labels helps you guide customers through their purchase decisions:

Old World (European) Labels

  • Focus on place of origin rather than grape variety
  • Classified by region (e.g., Bordeaux, Chianti) with regulatory quality levels
  • Often requires knowledge of which grapes are grown in which regions
  • Look for terms like AOC/AOP (France), DOC/DOCG (Italy), DO/DOCa (Spain)

New World Labels

  • Typically emphasize the grape variety (e.g., Cabernet Sauvignon, Chardonnay)
  • Clearly state the region and sometimes vineyard
  • More likely to include flavor descriptions and food pairing suggestions
  • Often more approachable for casual wine drinkers

QUICK SELLING TIP

When a customer is uncertain about a wine selection, offer to explain the label. This builds trust in your expertise and often leads to higher-value purchases.
